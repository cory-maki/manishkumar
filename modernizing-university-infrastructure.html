Modernizing the Administrative Spine of the Public University

📅 February 24, 2026
✎ Manish Kumar
⌚ 7 min read

Universities tend to underinvest in administrative infrastructure for as long as possible, then invest in it all at once under conditions of stress. Manish Kumar has led that work at multiple institutions, and the pattern is consistent enough that it is worth pulling apart.

The case for modernization is usually framed as efficiency: replace the older system, reduce manual reconciliation, free staff time for higher-value work. That case is real, but it is incomplete. The deeper case is that an institution's ability to answer questions about itself — about its costs, its compensation patterns, its research portfolio, its student outcomes — is bounded by the systems it uses to track itself. When those systems are old, the questions an institution can answer are old too.

The Workday Lesson

An enterprise-wide implementation of a system like Workday Financial and Human Capital Management is, in the language of project management, a multi-year effort. In the language of institutional change, it is also a referendum on how the institution actually works. The implementation surfaces every undocumented exception, every shadow process, every place where two units have agreed to disagree about how a transaction should be classified. There is no way to clean up those things at the end. They have to be cleaned up as you go, or the new system becomes a more expensive version of the old one.

The institutions that get the most out of an ERP transition are the ones that treat it as a chance to renegotiate how work flows — not a technology project at all, really, but a process project that happens to require new technology. The ones that treat it as a technology project tend to spend two years implementing and another three years explaining why the promised benefits did not materialize.

An institution's ability to answer questions about itself is bounded by the systems it uses to track itself. When those systems are old, the questions are old too.

What to Standardize, What to Leave Alone

The hardest design choice in any university modernization is what to standardize and what to leave alone. Universities have a deep cultural preference for local variation: the chemistry department genuinely does have different needs than the music school, and a CFO who flattens that variation in the name of process discipline will lose the trust of the academic units very quickly. But unbounded local variation produces systems that cannot be operated, audited, or improved at scale.

The working answer is to standardize aggressively at the data layer — definitions, categories, charts of accounts — and to allow flexibility at the workflow layer where local context actually matters. That distinction sounds technical, but it is really a governance question. It is also one of the places where the partnership between the CFO, the CIO, and the academic leadership matters most. This is part of the broader story of financial sustainability in higher education.

Modernization as a Trust Exercise

Underneath the technical work, modernization is fundamentally a trust exercise. Faculty and staff are being asked to learn new tools, abandon long-standing workarounds, and accept the temporary friction of transition on a promise that things will work better at the end. That promise has to be kept, in real visible ways, or the next modernization effort five years later starts from a deeper deficit of trust.

Mr. Kumar's preference, formed across the work at Bowie State, UNC Chapel Hill, Rutgers, and Northeastern Illinois, is to pair every major systems initiative with explicit, measurable commitments to the people doing the work — clearer reports, faster reimbursements, fewer reconciliations, less unpaid overtime at month-end. The modernization headlines tend to be about transparency and governance reporting. The lived experience of modernization, for the people inside the institution, has to be about their actual work getting easier. If that part is missing, the rest is theater. This view also informs his reading of how transformation efforts should be sequenced.

Conclusion

Administrative modernization is not glamorous. It does not produce ribbon-cuttings or naming opportunities or, usually, much external notice. It also happens to be the single most leveraged investment a public university can make in its own future, because it changes what the institution is capable of seeing about itself. Everything downstream — strategy, financial planning, academic decisions — gets better when the instruments are sharper.
