What Financial Sustainability Actually Means in Higher Education

📅 April 8, 2026
✎ Manish Kumar
⌚ 8 min read

Financial sustainability in higher education is one of those phrases that has been used so often it has nearly lost its meaning. In conversations with boards, faculty, and rating agencies, Manish Kumar has watched the term flatten into a euphemism for cost-cutting. It is something more demanding than that.

A sustainable institution is one that can fund its academic mission today and continue to fund it under reasonably foreseeable adverse conditions tomorrow. That definition sounds modest until you pull it apart. It assumes the institution has done the work of actually defining its mission with enough specificity to test resource decisions against it. It assumes leadership has built financial models that hold up under scrutiny rather than under optimism. And it assumes the board is willing to hear and act on the model's conclusions even when those conclusions are uncomfortable.

Beyond the Cost-Cutting Reflex

When an institution starts losing money, the first instinct is to cut. There are moments when cutting is the right answer, but they are rarer than the reflex suggests. More often, the underlying problem is structural: a revenue model misaligned with demographic reality, a cost base built around assumptions that no longer hold, or a portfolio of academic programs that has grown without ever being pruned. Cutting without addressing the structure produces a smaller version of the same problem.

The work that produced the four-notch Moody's upgrade at Northeastern Illinois University was not primarily about cuts. It was about restoring credibility — with the legislature, with rating agencies, with internal stakeholders — by showing that the institution could plan honestly, hit its targets, and explain its choices. Once credibility returned, capital returned. Once capital returned, the academic agenda could move again.

A sustainable institution is one that can fund its academic mission today and continue to fund it under reasonably foreseeable adverse conditions tomorrow. The rest is commentary.

The Three Hard Conversations

Genuine sustainability work tends to surface three conversations that institutions would prefer not to have. The first is about portfolio: which programs continue, which consolidate, which sunset. The second is about the cost of place: how much does it actually cost to operate this physical campus, and what would change if it operated differently. The third is about people: where the institution's compensation philosophy is competitive, where it is not, and what the consequences are either way.

These conversations are hard because each one cuts across constituencies that have good reasons to defend the status quo. The CFO's job is not to win the argument but to make sure the data on the table is honest, that the trade-offs are visible, and that the decision the board ultimately makes is informed by reality rather than aspiration. It is closer to facilitation than advocacy. The institutions that get the work right tend to share a disciplined approach to institutional transformation rather than a single charismatic intervention.

Sustainability and Mission Are the Same Project

There is an old framing in which financial discipline and academic mission sit in tension — as if rigor about money is a betrayal of higher education's purpose. That framing is wrong, and it has done real damage. The institutions that have most fully served their public mission over the past two decades are not the ones that ignored their balance sheets. They are the ones that took finance seriously enough to give the academic enterprise something durable to stand on.

This is the lens Manish Kumar brings to the work at Bowie State University and brought to UNC Chapel Hill, Rutgers, and Northeastern Illinois before it. Modern administrative and financial systems matter not because they are interesting in themselves, but because they are the substrate on which everything else — research, teaching, student success, public service — depends. When the substrate is solid, the academic mission has room to breathe.

Conclusion

Financial sustainability is not a finance problem. It is an institutional problem with financial expressions. Treating it as the former produces tactics; treating it as the latter produces strategy. The difference, over a decade, is the difference between an institution that survives and one that grows into the next version of itself.
